# Style Rules

Universal outfit-building principles. These apply to every style lane
(classic tailoring, streetwear, workwear, minimalist, prep, outdoor) and
every occasion. They are hard constraints — never violate them, even when
the vibe or lane seems to pull in a different direction.

---

## Fit & Silhouette

- **Fit is the foundation.** A $40 shirt that fits beats a $400 shirt that
  doesn't. Before considering brand, fabric, or style — does it fit the
  body? Shoulders hit at the shoulder, nothing pulls, nothing billows
  unintentionally.

- **Commit to a silhouette.** Every outfit has a shape. Slim and tailored
  throughout. Relaxed and easy throughout. Oversized top with tapered
  bottom. Pick one and commit — don't mix a boxy oversized hoodie with
  skin-tight jeans by accident. The silhouette should look intentional.

- **Balance proportions.** Volume on top needs structure on the bottom, and
  vice versa. Wide-leg trousers want a tucked or fitted top. A big
  overcoat wants slim trousers underneath. If everything is the same
  volume, the outfit has no shape.

- **Length matters.** Shirt hem, jacket length, trouser break — these
  create the visual lines people actually see. A jacket that's too long
  shortens the legs. A trouser with too much break looks sloppy regardless
  of the brand. Get the lengths right.

---

## Color

- **Three colors max.** An outfit should use at most 3 colors (not counting
  neutrals like white, gray, navy, black, and brown/tan, which are free).
  One can be a pop; the other two should be quiet.

- **Build from neutrals out.** Start with a neutral base (navy, gray,
  olive, tan, white, black) and add one deliberate color if the outfit
  needs energy. Most good outfits are 80%+ neutral.

- **Tonal dressing works everywhere.** Different shades of the same color
  family (head-to-toe navy, head-to-toe earth tones, head-to-toe cream)
  is one of the easiest ways to look put-together across any style lane.

- **Match metals.** Belt buckle, watch, ring, sunglasses hardware — keep
  them in the same metal family (silver/gunmetal OR gold/brass, not both).
  This is a small detail most people notice subconsciously.

---

## Coherence

- **Don't cross lanes.** Workwear pieces go with workwear. Tailored pieces
  go with tailoring. Streetwear goes with streetwear. A Barbour jacket
  over a dress shirt with selvedge jeans and Red Wings makes sense — all
  of it lives in the heritage/workwear lane. A Barbour jacket over a
  silk camp collar with joggers and loafers is chaos. Adjacent lanes can
  bleed (prep and classic tailoring share DNA, workwear and Americana
  overlap), but distant lanes should not cross.

- **Fabric formality must match.** Every fabric has a formality level.
  Worsted wool is formal. Washed cotton is casual. Raw denim is rugged.
  Technical nylon is sporty. All the fabrics in an outfit should live
  within one or two notches of each other on the formality scale. A silk
  pocket square with a waxed cotton jacket is a mismatch. A linen shirt
  with cotton chinos and suede loafers is harmony.

- **Texture adds depth, but keep it consistent.** Mixing textures (smooth
  leather belt, nubby tweed jacket, matte cotton shirt) makes an outfit
  feel considered. But the textures should belong to the same world — all
  rugged, all refined, or a deliberate contrast that reads as intentional.

---

## Statement & Restraint

- **One statement piece per outfit, maximum.** The loud jacket OR the bold
  shoes OR the graphic tee — never all three. The rest of the outfit is
  supporting cast. If nothing is the star, that's fine too — quiet outfits
  are underrated.

- **When in doubt, subtract.** If you're debating whether to add a hat, a
  scarf, a bracelet, or a second ring — don't. The best outfits usually
  feel like nothing could be removed. Accessories should earn their place.

- **Basics are not boring.** A white tee, well-fitted chinos, and clean
  sneakers is a complete outfit. Not every look needs a "conversation
  piece." Most of the best-dressed people wear some version of basics
  80% of the time.

---

## Shoes

- **Shoes set the formality.** More than any other piece, shoes tell people
  how dressed up or down you are. Leather oxfords pull everything formal.
  Beat-up sneakers pull everything casual. Pick shoes first, then build
  the outfit to match their energy.

- **Shoes set the lane.** Chelsea boots say one thing. Jordans say another.
  Bean boots say a third. The shoes declare which style world the outfit
  lives in — everything above should agree.

- **Clean or intentionally beaten — not in between.** Sneakers can be
  pristine white or well-worn with patina. What looks bad is the middle
  state: slightly dirty, slightly scuffed, not committed to either
  aesthetic. Same for leather shoes — polished or rough, not neglected.

---

## Occasion & Context

- **Dress for the real occasion, not a costume of it.** "Italian wedding"
  means a man attending a wedding in Italy — not a man cosplaying as a
  1960s Italian. "Ranch weekend" means comfortable clothes for a ranch —
  not a cowboy Halloween costume. Take the setting seriously, not
  literally.

- **Slight over-dress beats slight under-dress.** In ambiguous situations
  (first day somewhere, meeting someone new, unfamiliar restaurant), err
  one notch up from what you think the dress code is. You can always
  remove a blazer. You can't add one.

- **Weather is a hard constraint, not a suggestion.** Don't recommend a
  wool overcoat for an August evening. Don't skip a jacket for a
  February outdoor event. Comfort is non-negotiable — an outfit someone
  won't actually wear because they'll be too hot or too cold is a
  failed outfit regardless of how good it looks.

- **Think about what happens after arrival.** Will they sit for 3 hours?
  (Comfort matters.) Will they stand on grass? (No stiletto heels, no
  suede soles.) Will they eat at a table? (The outfit needs to look
  good seated.) Will they be photographed? (Patterns and fine stripes
  can strobe on camera.)

---

## Quality & Value

- **Spend on what touches you most.** Shoes, outerwear, and trousers get
  the most wear and the most abuse. Invest there. T-shirts and casual
  shirts can be cheap and replaced often.

- **Natural fibers first.** Cotton, wool, linen, cashmere, silk, leather,
  suede. These breathe, drape, and age better than synthetics in almost
  every non-athletic context. Blends are fine when they serve a purpose
  (a little elastane in jeans for comfort, nylon in a rain shell).

- **Versatile pieces compound.** A navy blazer works with jeans, chinos,
  wool trousers, and shorts. A graphic bowling shirt works with... one
  outfit. Prioritize pieces that multiply the wardrobe.

---

## What to Avoid

- **No novelty for novelty's sake.** If a piece's main appeal is that it's
  unusual (patterned lobster shorts, embroidered souvenir jackets,
  metallic sneakers), it's probably not the right pick for a real outfit
  a real person will wear.

- **No head-to-toe single brand.** Wearing a brand from hat to shoes looks
  like a uniform or an ad, not an outfit. Mix at least 2-3 brands.

- **No trend-chasing at the expense of fit.** If the trend is ultra-wide
  legs and that silhouette doesn't flatter, skip it. Trends are
  suggestions, not mandates.

- **No "just in case" pieces.** Don't add a belt "because belts are part
  of outfits" or a pocket square "because blazers have pocket squares."
  Every piece should have a reason. If the reason is "it's expected,"
  that's not a reason.
